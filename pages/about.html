I'm a PhD candidate in Madabhushi Lab at Georgia Institute of Technology and Emory University. My research focuses on developing clinically machine learning tools for medical imaging. My work has been published in Nature Reviews Clinical Oncology and the Journal of Bone and Mineral Research Plus. Prior to joining my PhD, I worked on radiology projects at the Indian Institute of Science, where I developed 3D ultrasound reconstruction algorithms. I also worked on biomedical signal processing at a healthcare startup in Bangalore, India. I hold a Master of Engineering from the University of Cincinnati and a Bachelor of Engineering from M.S. Ramaiah Institute of Technology.

I'm interested in how structure reveals meaning. In biology, structure often appears in images: patterns of cells, spatial organization, and subtle architectural differences that carry prognostic value. My work centers on translating those patterns into measurable signals that can inform treatment decisions while remaining grounded in biology.

I'm drawn to problems where methodological rigor matters. External validation, calibration, and clinical utility matter more to me than marginal gains in performance metrics. The Japanese concept of Ikigai beautifully explained in Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life resonated with me. The idea of aligning what you enjoy, what you do well, and what creates real value to the society felt deeply relatable. It shaped how I think about building work that is both technically rigorous and practically useful.

My path has moved across signal processing, medical imaging systems, and large multi-site datasets, but the motivation remains consistent: build systems that are clear, robust, and useful. I enjoy designing feature pipelines and survival modeling frameworks. Outside the lab, I like to read, cook South Indian food and occasionally paint and sing Carnatic songs.

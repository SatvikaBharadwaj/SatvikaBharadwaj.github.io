Secure Ultrasound: Portable Prenatal Imaging for Rural India

Junior Research Fellow, Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore, India (2018 – 2021)

Access to prenatal ultrasound imaging remains critically limited in rural India, where trained sonographers and diagnostic equipment are scarce. The Secure Ultrasound project addresses this gap by developing a display-less, portable, and secure ultrasound system that can be operated by healthcare workers with minimal scanning experience. My role centered on developing and implementing advanced algorithms for 3D fetal volume reconstruction from freehand 2D ultrasound sweeps, transforming unstructured, operator-dependent scans into coherent volumetric representations suitable for remote expert review.

I designed and optimized volume filling algorithms that address the core technical challenges of freehand 3D ultrasound: pixel-to-voxel mapping, interpolation of sparse data, and handling of probe motion artifacts. I conducted comprehensive studies on the factors that affect reconstruction quality (probe stability, scan speed, sweep duration, and transducer contact) and used these findings to devise obstetric scanning protocols that maximize volume completeness even when performed by novice users. The result is a system that decouples image acquisition from interpretation, allowing frontline workers to capture diagnostically useful volumes that trained clinicians can review remotely.

Beyond algorithm development, I designed ultrasound acquisition training programs for community healthcare workers, equipping them to perform basic prenatal scans independently. This work was published as Semi-blinded freehand 3D ultrasound with novice users at SPIE 2022. The project reflects a commitment to translational engineering: building technology that meets people where they are, rather than where the infrastructure is.

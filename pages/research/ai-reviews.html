Artificial Intelligence in the Medical Domain

PhD Student, Georgia Institute of Technology and Emory University, Atlanta, GA (2022 – present)

The rapid proliferation of AI-driven tools in clinical medicine has generated enormous enthusiasm, but also a growing need for honest, rigorous assessment of what these tools can and cannot deliver. I co-authored a perspective published in Nature Reviews Clinical Oncology (Impact Factor: 82.2) that critically examines the state of artificial intelligence in digital pathology. The piece calls for a reality check: while deep learning has demonstrated impressive performance on curated research datasets, the path to clinical deployment is fraught with challenges around generalizability, reproducibility, regulatory standards, and integration into pathology workflows.

In a separate review published in Clinical Breast Cancer, I surveyed the landscape of AI-based radiomics and computational pathology approaches in triple-negative breast cancer, a subtype that lacks targeted therapies and has historically been difficult to stratify. This work synthesizes the current evidence on image-derived biomarkers for prognosis and therapy response prediction, identifying both the most promising directions and the methodological gaps that limit clinical translation. Together, these reviews reflect my commitment to bridging the gap between algorithmic innovation and clinical utility.

These reviews are grounded in firsthand experience developing and validating computational models on clinical trial data. They are written not from the periphery, but from within the space, informed by the practical challenges of building tools that must work across institutions, scanners, and patient populations. The goal is to push the field toward more rigorous evaluation standards and clinically meaningful endpoints, ensuring that AI in medicine delivers on its promise rather than perpetuating hype.

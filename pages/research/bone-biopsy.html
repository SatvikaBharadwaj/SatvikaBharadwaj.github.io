Machine Learning-Driven Deep Phenotyping of Bone Biopsies

PhD Student, Georgia Institute of Technology and Emory University, Atlanta, GA (2022 – present)

Histomorphometric analysis of undecalcified bone biopsies is the gold standard for assessing bone turnover, yet it remains a labor-intensive and subjective process. I developed ADAM (Automated Digital phenotyping And Morphological texture analysis), a deep-learning pipeline that automates the segmentation and quantitative characterization of bone biopsy images. The system extracts a rich set of intensity, texture, fractal, and shape descriptors from segmented bone structures, allowing objective and reproducible classification of bone turnover states relevant to osteoporosis management.

The pipeline leverages deep neural networks for pixel-level segmentation of key histological compartments (mineralized bone, osteoid, and marrow space) followed by extraction of handcrafted morphological and texture features that capture the microarchitectural complexity of bone tissue. By combining deep learning with classical image analysis, the approach bridges the gap between automated segmentation and clinically interpretable phenotyping, producing features that pathologists can reason about and validate against established histomorphometric parameters.

This work was presented at The American Society for Bone and Mineral Research (ASBMR) Annual Meeting 2024, where it received the ASBMR Young Investigator Travel Grant. The manuscript, ADAM: Automated digital phenotyping and morphological texture analysis of bone biopsy images, was published in JBMR Plus in 2025. A provisional patent, Systems and Methods for Automated Digital Phenotyping and Analysis of Bone Biopsy Images Using Deep Learning, has been filed, reflecting the translational potential of this technology for clinical bone pathology.

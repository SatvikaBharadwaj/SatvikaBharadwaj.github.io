Ground Penetrating Radar for Disaster Relief Operations

Graduate Student, University of Cincinnati, Cincinnati, OH (2016 – 2018)

In the aftermath of natural disasters (earthquakes, building collapses, landslides) rapid triage of trapped victims is critical, yet conventional portable X-ray equipment is often impractical in unstable, resource-constrained field conditions. This project explored the feasibility of using the StructScan Mini, a portable ground penetrating radar (GPR) scanner, as an alternative imaging modality for identifying severe pelvic fractures (displacement greater than 2.5 cm) at disaster sites, where pelvic injuries are among the leading causes of hemorrhagic death.

I analyzed RADAR images acquired from both control and fractured pelvic phantoms, developing image processing workflows to extract structural signatures of fracture displacement. From these 2D radar scans, I generated 3D pelvic models that enable visualization of fracture geometry and severity. The work was conducted in collaboration with Cincinnati Children's Hospital Medical Center and the University of Cincinnati's Biomedical and Civil Engineering departments, combining expertise in electromagnetic wave propagation, biomedical signal processing, and clinical orthopedics.

While GPR is well established in geophysical and civil engineering applications, its use for in-vivo musculoskeletal assessment in emergency medicine represents a novel direction. This project demonstrated the potential of repurposing existing portable RADAR technology for rapid, non-ionizing skeletal screening in austere environments, laying the groundwork for further development of field-deployable diagnostic tools that could meaningfully improve disaster response outcomes.

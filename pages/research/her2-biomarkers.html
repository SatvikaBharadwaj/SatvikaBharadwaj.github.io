Image-Based Biomarkers to Improve Treatment Management in HER2+ Breast Cancer

PhD Student, Georgia Institute of Technology and Emory University, Atlanta, GA (2022 – present)

HER2-positive breast cancer remains one of the most clinically consequential subtypes, and while trastuzumab-based regimens have transformed outcomes, a significant proportion of patients do not achieve pathological complete response. Predicting who will respond, and who will not, is critical to sparing patients from ineffective therapy and its associated toxicity. In this work, I integrated data from the NSABP B-41 clinical trial with computational pathology to develop spatial immune biomarkers capable of distinguishing trastuzumab responders from non-responders.

The approach centers on extracting spatially resolved features from digitized H&E-stained tissue sections of pre-treatment biopsies. By characterizing the spatial arrangement and density of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes relative to tumor nests, I identified immune architecture patterns that are predictive of response to trastuzumab-based neoadjuvant therapy. These computationally derived biomarkers capture information that is not apparent in conventional histopathologic assessment, revealing spatial interactions between the immune microenvironment and tumor cells associated with treatment sensitivity.

This work was presented as a poster at the American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO) 2025 Annual Meeting and received the Conquer Cancer Merit Award 2025 from the Conquer Cancer Foundation.

The resulting manuscript, Computationally Derived Spatial Immune Signature Identifies Trastuzumab Responders in HER2+ Breast Cancer: NSABP B-41 Clinical Trial Validation, was published in Clinical Cancer Research. The study showed that a computationally derived spatial immune biomarker can identify a subgroup of patients with substantial benefit from trastuzumab-based therapy, supporting more precise treatment personalization in HER2+ breast cancer.

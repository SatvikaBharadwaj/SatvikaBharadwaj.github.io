Computer-Aided Diagnostic Screening for Fetal Cardiac Chamber Detection

Undergraduate Researcher, M.S. Ramaiah Institute of Technology, Bangalore, India (2013 – 2014)

Congenital heart defects are among the most common and consequential birth abnormalities, yet prenatal detection rates remain inconsistent, particularly in settings where access to experienced fetal echocardiographers is limited. This project developed a computer-aided diagnostic algorithm to automatically segment and quantify the chambers of the fetal heart from standard ultrasound views, with the goal of providing an objective screening tool that could assist clinicians in identifying cardiac malformations in-utero.

The algorithm processes four-chamber view ultrasound images to delineate the boundaries of the right ventricle (RV) and left ventricle (LV), computing their respective diameters and the RV-to-LV ratio. Deviations from normal ventricular proportions serve as quantitative indicators of structural abnormalities such as hypoplastic left heart syndrome, ventricular septal defects, and other chamber-level malformations. By reducing reliance on subjective visual assessment, the tool provides a reproducible, quantitative metric that can flag cases warranting further expert evaluation.

This early work, conducted during my undergraduate studies, established the foundation for my research trajectory at the intersection of medical imaging and computational analysis. It demonstrated the value of automated image quantification in clinical screening and reinforced the principle that has guided my subsequent work: building tools that augment clinical decision-making with objective, image-derived measurements.
